Glossary

Every term that might slow you down, explained.

Nobody should have to Google "DEG" to read a briefing written for them. This is the full plain-English dictionary of every industry-specific term used in these five pages. Alphabetical. No jargon.

A B C D E F H M N O P Q R S T V
A
Alpha entries
ACV — Actual Cash Value
"the market value of the car"
What the vehicle is worth on the used market right now, in its pre-loss condition. Insurers use this to decide whether to repair a damaged car or write it off. If the repair cost plus the salvage value exceeds ACV × a state-specific percentage, the car is a total loss.
ADAS — Advanced Driver Assistance Systems
"the safety sensors"
All the cameras, radars, and sensors that handle lane-keeping, automatic emergency braking, adaptive cruise, blind-spot warnings, etc. Almost every 2020+ vehicle has them. After a collision, most of these systems require recalibration before the car is legally and safely driveable.
Adjuster
"the insurance person on the claim"
The insurance company employee (or contractor) who reviews, approves, or rejects the repair estimate. The person your estimator is negotiating with every time a supplement goes back and forth.
ARMS — Automated Rental Management System
"Enterprise's shop portal"
The rental car system Enterprise uses. When a customer is in a rental while their car's being repaired, your shop logs into ARMS to authorize, extend, or close out the rental. Hertz and other rental companies have their own equivalent portals.
B
BEV — Battery Electric Vehicle
"a fully electric car"
A vehicle that runs only on battery — no gas engine at all. Teslas, Rivians, Mach-E, Ioniq 5, etc. Requires high-voltage safety procedures during any repair that touches structural or electrical components.
C
Calibration
"re-aiming the car's sensors"
After a collision repair, the ADAS sensors (cameras, radars) need to be precisely re-aligned or they'll malfunction. This is calibration. Sometimes done in-shop, often sent out to a specialty vendor (Elitek, Precision Diagnostics, AirPro). Required on roughly 60% of repairs in 2025+.
CCC ONE
"the estimating software"
The most widely used estimating software in the US collision industry — about 28,000 shops use it. Made by CCC Intelligent Solutions. "CCC ONE" is their core platform. Mitchell and Audatex/Solera are the other two major players.
D
DEG — Database of Estimator's Guide
"the independent rulings database"
An independent industry body that publishes rulings on which operations should be included or excluded from standard estimates. When you add a line to an estimate and the adjuster pushes back, DEG rulings are the evidence you cite. Free, public, updated constantly.
DRP — Direct Repair Program
"the insurance carrier contract"
A contract between your shop and an insurance carrier (State Farm, Allstate, Progressive, etc.) that refers claims to you in exchange for agreed-upon pricing, markup caps, and procedure rules. Most shops have 2–6 DRP relationships at a time. Each has different rules.
E
E01 / Total Loss
"the car's being written off"
When the cost to repair a vehicle exceeds a state-specific percentage of its actual cash value, the insurer writes it off rather than paying for repair. "E01" is a shorthand code used in estimating software to flag a probable total loss. Different states use different thresholds (typically 70–80%).
ECD — Estimated Completion Date
"when the car will be ready"
The date your shop promises the vehicle will be ready for the customer. When it slips, everyone — the customer, the rental company, the insurer — needs to be updated. One of the most common administrative pains in the business.
F
FNOL — First Notice of Loss
"when the claim opens"
The moment a customer tells their insurance company that something happened to their car. Usually includes a few photos, a description of the damage, and a police report. This is the earliest signal your shop can get about an incoming job — before the vehicle even arrives.
H
HV — High Voltage
"electric-car safety stuff"
Refers to the 200V+ battery systems in hybrids and electric vehicles. Repairing anywhere near an HV system without proper isolation, lock-out/tag-out, and PPE can kill a technician. Every EV repair requires documented HV safety procedures.
HIRS — Hertz Integrated Rental System
"Hertz's version of ARMS"
The rental car portal for Hertz. When a customer rents from Hertz during their repair, your shop manages the authorization and extensions through HIRS. Same idea as Enterprise's ARMS, different vendor, different login.
M
Mitchell
"another estimating software"
Mitchell International — one of the three major estimating software vendors (CCC, Mitchell, Audatex). Many shops use Mitchell instead of or alongside CCC, especially on the mechanical side of the business.
N
Not-Included Operation
"labor you have to add manually"
A repair operation that is NOT automatically included in the standard labor time published by the estimating software. Someone has to identify it and type it in as a separate line. Examples: masking, feather-prime-block, corrosion protection, post-repair test drive. These are where most "missed money" on estimates lives.
O
OEM — Original Equipment Manufacturer
"the car's manufacturer"
The company that made the vehicle — Ford, Toyota, BMW, etc. "OEM procedures" are the official repair instructions published by the manufacturer. Following OEM procedures is increasingly a legal and insurance requirement, especially on ADAS-equipped and EV vehicles.
P
P-Pages — Procedure Pages
"the software's labor-time rulebook"
Official documentation from CCC, Mitchell, and Audatex that defines exactly which operations are included in each standard labor time, and which require separate manual line entry. When an adjuster challenges a line, P-Pages are the first-line evidence.
PHEV — Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicle
"part gas, part electric"
A car that has both an internal combustion engine AND a battery system large enough to drive short distances purely on electricity. Requires HV safety procedures just like a full EV during repair. Common examples: Toyota RAV4 Prime, Ford Escape PHEV, Jeep Wrangler 4xe.
Q
Quarantine (EV Battery)
"50-foot safe zone around a damaged battery"
When an electric vehicle battery is damaged and at risk of thermal runaway (fire), OEM procedures require the vehicle to sit in a 50-foot isolation zone or in a specialized "ecology block" containment structure until it's declared safe. Typically rented at $85+/day.
R
Repair Order (RO)
"the job file"
The central document that tracks a single vehicle's repair from intake to delivery. Contains the estimate, supplements, parts orders, sublet invoices, notes, photos, everything. Also called a "workfile" in some software.
Routine (Claude Code Routine)
"Anthropic's term for a scheduled AI agent"
The specific Anthropic platform feature this whole product is built on. A Routine is an AI agent that runs automatically in response to a trigger — a scheduled time, an API call, an incoming webhook. Released publicly in April 2026. Every agent in this hub is a Routine.
S
Secure Share (CCC)
"how CCC shares estimate data"
CCC's API platform for allowing third-party tools (like these agents) to read and write estimate data in CCC ONE. The authorized integration path — no screen scraping, no workarounds.
Sublet
"work outsourced to a specialty vendor"
Any repair work you send to an outside vendor — typically ADAS calibration, glass replacement, mechanical alignment, or specialized diagnostics. The vendor sends you a PDF invoice; you add it as a sublet line on the repair order (with your allowed markup).
Supplement
"an addition to the original estimate"
When additional damage or additional operations are discovered after the original estimate, you submit a supplement. Common after teardown. Adjusters approve or push back on supplements constantly. A big source of the "estimating battlefield."
T
Teardown
"taking the car apart to see the real damage"
The process of removing panels, components, and trim to reveal hidden damage that wasn't visible in the initial estimate photos. Takes 2–8 hours depending on severity. You can't properly write a complete estimate without doing it — but if the car totals out, the teardown labor is often disputed.
Trigger
"the event that starts the agent"
Any observable event in your shop that the agent can listen for — a new estimate saved, an email received, a VIN decoded, a date changed. The core thesis of this whole product: triggers are what make AI useful, not the AI itself.
V
VIN — Vehicle Identification Number
"the car's 17-character fingerprint"
Every vehicle has a unique 17-character code that identifies the year, make, model, trim, engine, assembly plant, and more. "VIN decode" means running those 17 characters through a lookup to pull back all the vehicle's specs — including whether it's a hybrid, EV, or has specific ADAS equipment.
