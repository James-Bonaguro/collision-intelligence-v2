The Agents

Five agents. One system. Each replaces a specific recurring task.

Each agent listens for one specific event in your shop — a new assignment, a sublet invoice, a schedule slip — and then does the work that would normally take a human fifteen minutes. The flagship agent, the one most shops start with, is first.

★ Start Here · Flagship Agent

It tells you which cars are going to total — before you tear them down.

When a new assignment lands in your queue, the agent looks at the vehicle, the damage photos, the car's value, and your state's total-loss rules — and tells you whether teardown is worth it. If it's borderline, it pre-writes the teardown labor lines so you get paid even when the car totals.

What triggers it

A new repair assignment drops in your estimating software. Or a customer uploads first-notice-of-loss photosFNOL — the initial photos the customer or insurer submits when a claim is opened, before the car even arrives at your shop. through the carrier's app. The agent wakes up automatically.

What it actually does

It pulls the car's actual cash value in real-time from market data. It estimates the repair cost from the assignment photos. It checks your state's total-loss threshold. It does the math: is this car going to get written off?

If the answer is "probably yes," it drops a Slack message into your estimator's channel: "Hold on this BMW X5 — likely total loss. I've pre-staged teardown lines in case you proceed." If they proceed anyway, the teardown compensation is already in the estimate — not a fight later.

Why this one first

Because it's the single most visceral pain in the business. Every shop owner has a story about four hours of teardown they never got paid for. This agent ends that story.

"I'll stop you from wasting six hours on a car that's going to be totaled anyway."
Time saved: 3–6 hrs per flagged vehicle
Recovered: $7,480/mo average
Setup: 1 week
The Other Four
Agent · Estimate Scrubber

It finds the money your estimator left on the estimate.

Every time your estimator saves a draft estimate, the agent cross-references it against the official labor-time rules and independent industry rulings — and adds the lines that should be there, with citations the adjuster can't reject.

What triggers it

Your estimator hits "save" on a draft estimate or supplement in your estimating software. That's it.

What it actually does

It reads the estimate. It decodes the VIN. It pulls the list of operations the estimator wrote. It compares them against the official P-PagesProcedure Pages — the official rule documents from CCC, Mitchell, and Audatex. They specify exactly which operations are and aren't included in standard labor times. and the DEG databaseDatabase of Estimator's Guide — independent industry rulings on which operations require manual line entry.. It identifies the missing lines — masking, feather-prime-block, seat-belt inspection, hazardous material, whatever applies.

It pushes those lines back into the estimate with a one-line justification citing the rule. The adjuster sees: "Feather-prime-block: DEG Ruling 2023-014, required for color match on this substrate." Not "please approve."

"I'll find the money you're leaving on every estimate — before you submit it."
Recovered: $300–$800 per estimate
Monthly: $28,050 average shop
Setup: 2 weeks
Agent · Status Sync

When a job slips, it handles the rental, the insurer, and the customer.

Every time a completion date changes on a job in your shop, the agent fans out the update — extending the rental, posting a delay note to the insurer, and texting the customer — automatically, simultaneously, without anyone logging into a portal.

What triggers it

Your estimator changes the completion date on a job. Parts delay, calibration reschedule, supplement approval lag — whatever the reason.

What it actually does

It authenticates into Enterprise ARMSARMS — the rental company Enterprise's portal for collision shops. Where shops update rental authorizations for customers driving loaners during repair. and Hertz's portal. Extends the rental end date. Inputs the delay reason code. Posts a note in the insurer's portal. Sends the customer an SMS: "Hey, John — your Toyota's going to be a few days late. New pickup: Tuesday the 24th. Your rental's extended, no action needed."

Total human time: zero. Total elapsed time: forty seconds.

"Every time a job slips, I handle the rental extension, the insurance note, and the customer text. Automatically."

Agent · Sublet Reconciler

It turns calibration PDFs into line items automatically.

When a sublet vendor emails you an invoice, the agent reads the PDF, calculates your allowed markup under the insurance carrier's DRP agreement, and posts the line items into your estimate with the PDF attached — before your estimator even opens the email.

What triggers it

A PDF invoice arrives in your shop's email from Precision Diagnostics, Elitek, AirPro, or any other sublet vendor on a known list.

What it actually does

It OCRs the PDF. Extracts the VIN, the operations performed, the cost. Matches it to the open repair order. Queries your DRP contractDirect Repair Program contract — the agreement between your shop and an insurance carrier that specifies allowed markups, pricing caps, and procedural rules. for the allowed markup percentage. Creates the line items in your estimating software. Attaches the original PDF as documentation.

Your estimator's first awareness of the invoice is a notification that says: "Precision Diagnostics invoice processed, $340 added to RO-4827, PDF attached." They glance. They approve. Done.

"I'll take every calibration PDF that hits your inbox and turn it into a line item before you see it."
Time saved: 15 min per invoice
Monthly value: $430
Setup: 1 week
Agent · EV Compliance Documenter

It generates the EV safety paperwork your tech would otherwise skip.

When an electric or hybrid vehicle checks in, the agent generates a VIN-specific high-voltage safety checklist, sends it to the tech's tablet, and auto-adds the required safety labor lines to the estimate — so the compliance paperwork actually happens.

What triggers it

A vehicle is checked in at intake and the VIN decodes to a hybrid, plug-in hybrid, or battery-electric vehicle.

What it actually does

It decodes the VIN to identify the exact make, model, and high-voltage system. It pulls the OEM's published safety procedures — high-voltage isolation, lock-out/tag-out, thermal event response, quarantine distance. It generates a digital checklist customized to that vehicle and sends it to the assigned tech's mobile device.

It also adds the corresponding safety labor lines to the estimate automatically — HV inspection, isolation procedure, quarantine documentation if applicable — so you actually get paid for the safety steps the OEM requires you to perform.

"Every EV that rolls in gets a proper safety workflow — not a guess. And you bill for every step."
Added to estimate: $280 per EV
Liability: documented
Setup: 3 weeks
